Problems with machine translation

Although machine translation has significantly improved over the last decade to the point of excelling at straightforward texts written in standard language, it cannot fully replace human translators. There are still instances where the use of machine translation is not advisable, due to many reasons, some of them include:

- Privacy concerns – MT engines work by storing translations for future use. It means that all the data is available to the providers of the tool. This poses a risk, especially for sensitive information like legal documents, where confidentiality is very important. To give an example - lawyers using free translation tools may inadvertently expose confidential information.

- Poor translation of low-resource languages - Neural machine translation may give poor results for languages with limited training data. Some language models struggle to perform if they lack exposure to language nuances, leading to errors in translation.. In such cases, human translators are more effective in capturing the correct meaning.

- Lack of specialized knowledge – One of the biggest risk of using MT translation includes mistranslation of specialized terminology, like medical or legal jargon. Mistakes in critical texts can have serious consequences as human safety may depend on poorly translated decisive information. It can be concluded that - the more important the text, the less use of MT is recommended, which only highlights the importance of human expertise in the field of translation.

- Culturally inappropriate output – Machine translation engines are often unaware of cultural subtleties and make texts culturally inappropriate, leading to unintended meanings or faux pas. A similar situation happened to American Airlines who wanted to promote their new leather seats in Mexico. The slogan “Fly in leather” was machine-translated as ”Vuela en cuero”, which in some Spanish-speaking regions means “Fly naked”. This unfortunate accident caused a lot of embarrassment to the company and ruined its reputation.

- Errors:

a) Proper nouns - machine translation tools frequently mistranslate proper nouns, often providing literal renderings that can confuse readers. A notable incident took place in 2021 when the Spanish Ministry of Industry utilized machine translation for a text mentioning a person named Dolores del Campo. The resulting translation, “Pain of Field,” vividly illustrates the challenges machine translation faces in accurately handling proper nouns.”

b) Idioms – some idiomatic expressions are still challenging for MT systems - there is a risk that they may be misinterpreted and translated literally, losing their intended meaning in the process. For lesser-known, Polish idioms such as “Gdyby kózka nie skakała, to by nóźki nie złamała" – DeepL, Google Translate and Chat gpt offer literal translation: “If the goat hadn't jumped, it wouldn't have broken its leg,” instead of the idiomatic “better safe than sorry.” Similarly, translating the English idiom “bee’s knees,” meaning “the best thing,” into Polish results in the literal translation “kolana pszczół” (knees of the insects).

c) Genders and pronouns – While neural machine translation can process sentences, it does not fully comprehend them. For instance, the French sentence: “Dans leur maison, il y a sa voiture et sa voiture, ses serviettes et ses serviettes, et sa bibliothèque et la sienne” is inaccurately translated into English as “In their house there is his car and his car, his towels and his towels, and his library and his.” (It should be “her car and his car, her towels and his towels.”) The program’s inability to discern context leads to the repetitive use of the word “his” instead of adjusting pronouns based on the item possessed. This highlights a challenge for MT systems, especially in languages like French where pronouns must align with the possessed item rather than the possessor.

d) Ambiguity and cultural bias – Certain phrases and words can carry multiple meanings and it falls upon machine translation systems to choose the best most appropriate one. For example, when translating the English sentence: “Today I played soccer and built a house,” Google Translate renders it as “Dzisiaj grałem w piłkę nożną i zbudowałem dom.” The use of masculine forms like “grałem” and “zbudowałem” suggests a bias toward the stereotype that soccer and house-building are typically associated with men. On the other hand, the sentence “Today I baked a cake and played with my child,” DeepL translates as “Dziś upiekłam ciasto i bawiłam się z dzieckiem.” The feminine forms “upiekłam” and “bawiłam się” imply a bias toward the stereotype that baking and childcare are mostly women’s activities.

While machine translation is a valuable tool, it has its limitations. Therefore, for sensitive texts that require understanding of language nuances and context, human translation is recommended. Humans can grasp the deeper meaning, cultural nuances and make sure that the tone is appropriate for the target audience.
